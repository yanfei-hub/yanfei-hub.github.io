My research is motivated by a simple question: how can we make longitudinal clinical data more useful for real decision making without pretending that routine care data behave like clean trial data?

## Themes

### Causal inference for longitudinal healthcare data

I develop methods for causal inference using electronic health records, claims, and other real-world data sources. A recurring focus is how to define treatment, eligibility, follow-up, and comparison groups in ways that remain clinically meaningful when exposures evolve over time.

### Target trial emulation and treatment strategy evaluation

I am especially interested in target trial emulation for settings with treatment initiation, persistence, switching, gaps, restarts, and add-on strategies. This line of work aims to produce effect measures that align with clinical decisions rather than relying only on simplified baseline contrasts.

### Interpretable longitudinal modeling

My work also studies interpretable disease progression models and trustworthy machine learning systems for healthcare. I focus on model structure, uncertainty, calibration, and the relationship between predictive signals and causal or decision-oriented questions.

### Bias-aware real-world evidence

Because routine clinical data are shaped by care processes, my research pays close attention to confounding, censoring, selection, irregular observation, and transportability. The goal is not only estimation, but also honest diagnosis of where real-world evidence is and is not reliable.

## Application areas

- Alzheimer's disease and neurodegeneration
- Cancer outcomes and treatment safety
- Cardiovascular adverse events
- Chronic disease progression
- Drug repurposing using longitudinal clinical data
